The Eclipse

A total solar eclipse over the Cantabrian coast, 12 August 2026 — the reason for the timing.

12 August 2026 · Cantabrian coast · totality ~20:27, about a minute

The one fixed point the whole trip is built around. For roughly a minute, the Moon completely covers the Sun, the sky goes dark in the early evening, and the corona appears. A 99% partial eclipse is a different, lesser thing — so being inside the path of totality, with a clear view, is everything.

Where

The path of totality crosses northern Spain coast-to-coast. We’ll be on the Cantabrian coast around Santander, ideally at a west/north-west-facing spot near Comillas or San Vicente de la Barquera — open sea horizon, slightly longer totality.

The catch — a very low Sun

In Spain the eclipse happens about an hour before sunset, so the Sun sits low on the western horizon. You need an unobstructed view out over the sea — no hills, no headland in the way. This is why a west-facing coastal viewpoint matters, and why deep valleys (like the Pyrenees) are no good for it.

Timing on the day

  • ~19:30 — partial phase begins (the Moon starts to bite into the Sun). Glasses on.
  • ~20:27 — totality. Glasses off for the ~1 minute it’s total. Look up.
  • after — partial phase continues toward sunset.

Exact local times vary by a minute or two along the coast — confirm for the chosen viewpoint nearer the time.

Safety — eclipse glasses are essential

Looking at the partial phases without certified eclipse glasses (ISO 12312-2) damages your eyes. Buy several pairs before the trip — they sell out near eclipse dates. Glasses come off only during the brief total phase. Bring a pair for Freya and supervise. Added to the checklist.

Plan B — clouds

The north coast can grey over in August. We’ve got buffer days either side of the 12th, so the plan stays flexible: be ready to move along the coast that afternoon to find a clear western horizon.

On the same evening

A chamber concert is on in the church at San Vicente de la Barquera as part of the Festival Internacional de Santander — the eclipse comes first.
