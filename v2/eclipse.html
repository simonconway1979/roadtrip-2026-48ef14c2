The Eclipse

A total solar eclipse over the Cantabrian coast, 12 August 2026 — the reason for the timing.

12 August 2026 · Cantabrian coast · totality ~20:27, about a minute

The one fixed point the whole trip is built around. For roughly a minute, the Moon completely covers the Sun, the sky goes dark in the early evening, and the corona appears. A 99% partial eclipse is a different, lesser thing — so being inside the path of totality, with a clear view, is everything.

Where we’ll be

We’re based at Pechón, on the far-western Cantabrian coast — a clifftop peninsula 10 minutes from San Vicente de la Barquera, with a west-facing horizon over the Bay of Biscay. That puts the eclipse, quite literally, on our doorstep.

At totality the Sun sits ~9° up in the west-north-west, over the sea — so we want an open WNW horizon. Three viewpoints within minutes, in order of preference:

  1. The Pechón clifftop miradores — right by the base, west-facing. Scout the exact spot on arrival.
  2. San Vicente de la Barquera (~10 min) — the eclipsed Sun over the estuary with the Picos de Europa behind. Potentially the most beautiful framing.
  3. Oyambre headland / dunes (~25 min east) — wide-open coast if we want elbow room.

The catch — a very low Sun

In Spain the eclipse happens about an hour before sunset, so the Sun sits low on the western horizon. You need an unobstructed view out over the sea — no hill or headland in the way. This is why the west-facing clifftop matters, and why a single obstruction can hide the corona entirely.

The plan for the day — 12 August

A deliberately gentle day, with everything banked for the evening.

  • Daytime — keep it low-key: a San Vicente potter, a beach, an early dinner. Don’t over-program; the evening is the event. Prep a picnic / takeaway to carry up.
  • ~18:30–19:00 — drive to the chosen viewpoint and claim a spot early (the coast will be busy with eclipse-watchers). Blanket, picnic, layers — it cools noticeably as totality nears.
  • ~19:30 — partial phase begins; the Moon starts to bite into the Sun. Glasses on. Freya’s pair on and supervised.
  • ~20:27 — totality, about a minute. Glasses off — look up at the corona, the horizon glow, the sudden dusk.
  • after — the partial phase plays out toward a coastal sunset. Glasses back on.

Pack for the evening: certified glasses for all three, a blanket, warm layers, snacks/picnic, and a phone tripod for the wide shot (don’t spend totality fiddling with a camera — watch it).

Exact local times vary by a minute or two along the coast — confirm for the chosen viewpoint nearer the time.

Safety — eclipse glasses are essential

Looking at the partial phases without certified eclipse glasses (ISO 12312-2) damages your eyes. Buy several pairs before the trip — they sell out near eclipse dates. Glasses come off only during the brief total phase. Bring a pair for Freya and supervise. Added to the checklist.

Plan B — clouds

The north coast can grey over in August. We’ve got buffer days either side of the 12th, so the plan stays flexible: be ready to move along the coast that afternoon to find a clear western horizon.
